The Birth of Modern Robotics

1900s

The 20th century marked the emergence of modern robotics as a distinct field, characterized by new terminology, theoretical foundations, and practical implementations.

A. Term “Robot” Introduced (1920)

The word "robot" was coined by Czech writer Karel Čapek in his 1920 play R.U.R. (Rossum's Universal Robots). The term derives from the Czech word robota, meaning forced labor or drudgery. Čapek’s play popularized the idea of artificial beings created to serve humans, laying a conceptual foundation for robotics as we understand it today.

B. Isaac Asimov

In the 1940s, science fiction author Isaac Asimov was credited by the Oxford English Dictionary for first using the term robotics. He proposed the famous Three Laws of Robotics:

  1. Robots must never harm human beings.
  2. Robots must follow instructions from humans without violating rule 1.
  3. Robots must protect themselves without violating the other rules.

C. Cybernetics and Control Theory

In the 1940s and 1950s, American mathematician Norbert Wiener established the field of cybernetics — the study of control and communication in animals, humans, and machines. He introduced the concept of feedback loops, where systems self-regulate using output as input to maintain stability or enhance performance.

Wiener’s 1948 book Cybernetics or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine formalized these ideas, which had major impacts on robotics, AI, neuroscience, and systems engineering.

D. Unimate (1956)

The first practical industrial robot, Unimate, was developed by George Devol and Joseph Engelberger in 1956. It was an electro-mechanical robotic arm designed for factory automation tasks like die-casting and welding.

Unimate was installed at a General Motors plant in 1961 and became the first robot to perform repetitive tasks in manufacturing, launching the era of industrial robotics.

These key milestones—Čapek’s introduction of the word “robot,” Asimov’s conceptual laws, Wiener’s cybernetics, and Unimate’s real-world implementation—together define the birth of modern robotics as a fusion of concept, theory, and engineering innovation.
